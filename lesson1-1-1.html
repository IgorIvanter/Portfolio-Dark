Lesson 1.
Simple phrases

Introduction

This is your first lesson. Right now you're just starting out and you have a lot to learn to start speaking German fluently. Make sure to practice regularly, since it's the most important part of it that will determine your success. Also, always do the excercises provided in the lesson. They will help you better understand and apply what you've learned in the lesson. Try to squeeze as much information as humanly possible from this resource.

Good luck!

The very first task that you face is the study of fairly simple and very common German words and expressions. They will help not only to greet the German and say goodbye to him, but also to draw your attention to the pronunciation and the alphabet – those things that you will have to deal with constantly while learning a new foreign language. Try to rewrite every word as it is indicated in the lesson.

Before we start writing out and learning the first phrases, take a look at an interestin feature of German writing:

Every first letter of a noun is capitalized. This will help you distunguish between nouns and verbs, for example.

Ich habe ein Auto. Der Mann ist groß.

I have a car. The man is tall.

Now let's proceed directly to the main topic of the first section – the study of simple German phrases. Write out, speak and remember the expressions below, imagining that you are talking to a real person.

Greetings

Guten Morgen!
Good morning!

Guten Tag!
Good afternoon!

Guten Abend!
Good evening!

Notice that the first letter in the words Morgen, Tag and Abend is always capital, since these words are all nouns.

Questions

Wie geht es dir?
How are you?

Wie geht's?
How are you?

These two phrases have the same meaning.

Cultural note: in Germany there is a formal and an informal form (as in the exampleas above) of You. Most of the times, the informal one du is appropriate. More about that later on.

Answers

Gut, danke. Und dir?
Good, thank you. And you?

Perfekt! Und dir?
Perfect! And you?

Sehr gut
Very good

Schlecht / sehr schlecht
Bad / very bad

Es geht.
I'm fine.

Getting to know a new person

Wie heißt du?
What's your name?

Wer bist du?
What's your name?

Ich bin Jack.
I'm Jack.

Mein Name ist Jack. Und wer bist du?
My name's Jack, and what's yours?

These different ways to introduce yourself are completely interchangable.

Country of origin

Woher kommst du?
Where are you from?

Kommst du aus Deutschland?
Are you from Germany?

Ich komme aus Berlin. Und du?
I'm from Berlin. And you?

Say goodbye

Tschüs!
Bye!

Auf Wiedersehen.
See you later.

Bis bald!
See you!
